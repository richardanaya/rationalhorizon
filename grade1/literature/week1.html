Week 1 Overview

This classic tale introduces students to the power of literature while teaching important life lessons about hard work, preparation, and making wise choices. Through "The Three Little Pigs," children will explore story elements, develop listening skills, and discuss how the characters' different approaches to building their homes led to very different outcomes.

Learning Objectives

  • Listen actively to a complete story read aloud
  • Identify story elements (characters, setting, problem, solution)
  • Retell the story in correct sequence using key details
  • Compare and contrast the three pigs' choices and outcomes
  • Connect story lessons to real-life situations
  • Use story vocabulary in discussions and activities
  • Express opinions about characters and their actions

Materials Needed This Week

  • Multiple versions of "The Three Little Pigs" (picture books)
  • Story sequencing cards or pictures
  • Construction materials: straw, sticks, blocks
  • Character masks or puppets
  • Chart paper for story maps
  • Art supplies for illustrations
  • Audio recording of the story (if available)
  • Dramatic play props
  • Story journals for responses

Daily Lesson Plans

Monday: First Reading and Story Elements

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What do you need to build a strong house?"

  • Children share ideas about building houses
  • Introduce the story: "Today we'll meet three pigs who built very different houses"
  • Set listening purpose: "Listen to find out what each pig used to build"
Story Reading (20 minutes)

Interactive Read-Aloud:

  • Read "The Three Little Pigs" with expression and engagement
  • Show pictures clearly to all children
  • Pause for predictions: "What do you think will happen?"
  • Emphasize repetitive phrases: "I'll huff and I'll puff..."
  • Allow natural reactions and comments

Immediate Response:

  • "What did you think of that story?"
  • "Which pig was your favorite? Why?"
  • "What surprised you?"
Story Elements Introduction (15 minutes)

Identifying Basic Elements:

  • Characters: "Who was in the story?" (three pigs, wolf)
  • Setting: "Where did the story happen?" (countryside, forest)
  • Problem: "What was the big problem?" (wolf wanted to eat pigs)
  • Solution: "How was the problem solved?" (brick house protected them)

Create simple story map on chart paper with children's input

Closing (5 minutes)

Preview: "Tomorrow we'll act out the story ourselves!"

Tuesday: Story Retelling and Sequencing

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Quick Review: "Who remembers what the three pigs built their houses with?"

  • Review: straw, sticks, bricks
  • Practice story phrase: "I'll huff and I'll puff and I'll blow your house in!"
  • Preview: "Today we'll tell the story in order"
Sequence Activities (20 minutes)

Story Sequencing Cards:

  • Show picture cards of key story events (mixed up)
  • Children help put them in correct order
  • Use sequence words: first, next, then, finally
  • Retell story using ordered pictures

Three Houses Activity:

  • Show real materials: straw, small sticks, blocks
  • Let children build miniature houses
  • Test durability: which is strongest?
  • Connect to story: "Why did the brick house survive?"
Dramatic Retelling (15 minutes)

Acting Out the Story:

  • Assign roles: three pigs and wolf
  • Use simple props or gestures
  • Encourage dialogue from the story
  • Let multiple groups perform
  • Focus on sequence: what happens first, next, last?
Closing (5 minutes)

Reflect: "What was your favorite part to act out?"

Wednesday: Character Analysis and Choices

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Question of the Day: "Which pig made the best choice? Why?"

  • Children share opinions about the pigs' choices
  • Encourage reasoning: "I think... because..."
  • Accept different viewpoints with discussion
Character Comparison (20 minutes)

Three Pigs Comparison Chart:

  • First Pig: Quick, used straw, house blew down, had to run
  • Second Pig: Medium speed, used sticks, house blew down, had to run
  • Third Pig: Worked hard, used bricks, house stayed strong, stayed safe

Discussion Questions:

  • "Why did the first two pigs choose easier materials?"
  • "Why did the third pig work harder?"
  • "What happened because of their different choices?"
  • "Which pig would you rather be? Why?"
Real-Life Connections (15 minutes)

Hard Work in Our Lives:

  • "When do we need to work hard like the third pig?"
  • Examples: cleaning room thoroughly, practicing skills, doing homework carefully
  • "What happens when we rush through things?"
  • "What happens when we take time to do things well?"
  • Children share personal examples
Closing (5 minutes)

Personal reflection: "Think of one thing you want to do carefully like the third pig"

Thursday: Story Variations and Creative Response

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What if the pigs built houses from different materials?"

  • Brainstorm other building materials: glass, paper, metal, ice
  • Predict: "Would these materials be strong or weak?"
  • Encourage creative thinking
Story Variation Reading (15 minutes)

Different Version:

  • Read an alternative version of "The Three Little Pigs"
  • Compare to original: "What was the same? What was different?"
  • Discuss: "Which version did you like better? Why?"
  • Notice: stories can be told in different ways
Creative Art Activity (20 minutes)

Designing Our Own Houses:

  • Children draw their own version of the three houses
  • Include details about materials used
  • Add the characters: pigs and wolf
  • Write simple sentences about their drawings
  • Share creative house designs with class
Closing (5 minutes)

Gallery walk: admire everyone's creative house designs

Friday: Story Celebration and Assessment

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What lesson did the three little pigs teach us?"

  • Children share the story's main lesson
  • Discuss: hard work, preparation, making wise choices
  • Connect to their own experiences
Story Assessment Activity (20 minutes)

Individual Story Check:

  • Retelling: Each child retells the story to teacher or partner
  • Sequencing: Put picture cards in correct order
  • Character Understanding: Explain why third pig was smartest
  • Personal Connection: Share how story lesson applies to their life

While Others: Independent story activities

  • Coloring story characters
  • Building with blocks
  • Looking at other pig story books
Story Celebration (15 minutes)

Three Little Pigs Festival:

  • Final dramatic performance with costumes/masks
  • Display all artwork and story responses
  • Sing "Who's Afraid of the Big Bad Wolf?" (if known)
  • Vote on favorite pig and explain choices
  • Take photos of story displays
Closing (5 minutes)

Preview next week: "Next week we'll meet another hardworking character - The Little Red Hen!"

Assessment and Evaluation

What to Look For

  • Listening Comprehension: Does the child follow the story and understand key events?
  • Story Elements: Can the child identify characters, setting, and main problem?
  • Sequencing: Does the child retell events in correct order?
  • Character Analysis: Can the child compare the pigs' choices and outcomes?
  • Personal Connections: Does the child relate story lessons to real life?
  • Vocabulary: Does the child use story words in discussions?

Success Indicators

Meets Expectations:

  • Listens attentively during story reading
  • Identifies main characters and basic problem
  • Retells story with 3-4 key events in order
  • Understands that third pig made best choice
  • Participates in story discussions and activities

Exceeds Expectations:

  • Retells complete story with rich details
  • Explains why each pig made different choices
  • Makes sophisticated connections to real life
  • Uses story vocabulary accurately in discussions
  • Demonstrates creative thinking about story variations

Extension Activities for Home

Family Story Engagement

  • Story Retelling: Tell the story to family members using own words
  • Building Challenge: Build houses from household materials and test strength
  • Hard Work Discussion: Talk about times when hard work paid off in family life
  • Other Versions: Find different versions of the story at library
  • Character Comparison: Discuss which family members are most like each pig
  • Life Lessons: Share family stories about preparation and hard work

Connecting to Objectivist Principles

Using Your Mind (Reason)

Children analyze cause and effect in the story: the third pig thought ahead, worked harder, and achieved better results. They learn to evaluate characters' choices logically and understand that decisions have predictable consequences.

Learning Real Things (Reality)

The story's lesson reflects real-world principles: quality materials and careful work create stronger results than hasty shortcuts. Children connect the fictional tale to actual experiences with building, preparing, and working hard.

Learning Helps Me (Self-Interest)

Children see how the story's lesson benefits them personally: when they work carefully on projects, prepare for challenges, and choose quality over speed, they achieve better outcomes and feel proud of their accomplishments.

I Am Important (Individual Worth)

Each child's interpretation and response to the story is valued. Whether they relate to the hardworking third pig or understand the first two pigs' desire for quick results, every perspective contributes to rich class discussions about character and choice.
