Week 1 Overview

This foundational week helps students understand their own identity and what makes them unique individuals. Children will explore their personal characteristics, interests, and abilities while learning to appreciate both similarities and differences among classmates. This builds the foundation for understanding how individuals contribute to families, communities, and our nation.

Learning Objectives

  • Describe personal characteristics (appearance, interests, abilities)
  • Identify what makes them unique and special as individuals
  • Recognize similarities and differences among classmates
  • Express their preferences and explain why they like certain things
  • Understand basic personal information (full name, age, address)
  • Appreciate individual worth and the value of being themselves

Materials Needed This Week

  • Mirrors for self-observation
  • Construction paper and art supplies
  • Magazines for cutting pictures
  • Photo of each child (if available)
  • Chart paper for class comparisons
  • "All About Me" worksheets
  • Measuring tape or growth chart
  • Crayons and markers
  • Glue sticks and scissors

Daily Lesson Plans

Monday: What I Look Like

Opening Circle (5 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What color are your eyes?"

  • Children look in mirrors to observe eye color
  • Share eye colors around the circle
Physical Characteristics and Self-Portrait (12 minutes)

Mirror Observations: Each child uses mirror to observe hair color, eye color, height

Compare: "How are we the same? How are we different?"

Drawing Activity: Children draw self-portraits using mirrors, focusing on eye color and hair color

Closing (3 minutes)

Gallery walk: admire each other's self-portraits and notice details

Tuesday: Things I Like and Dislike

Opening Circle (5 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What is your favorite food?"

  • Each child shares their favorite food
  • Create a class list of favorite foods
  • Notice: "We all like different things!"
  • Discuss: "It's okay to have different favorites"
Preferences Exploration (12 minutes)

Like/Dislike Sorting:

  • Foods: pizza, broccoli, ice cream, carrots
  • Activities: reading, running, drawing, singing
  • Animals: dogs, cats, birds, fish
  • Colors: red, blue, green, yellow, purple

Individual Choice Charts:

  • Each child creates "My Favorites" chart
  • Draw or cut pictures of favorite things
  • Explain why they like each thing
  • Share one favorite with the class
Comparison Activity (12 minutes)

Same and Different:

  • Find someone with the same favorite color
  • Find someone with a different favorite food
  • Discuss: "Is it okay to like different things?"
  • Emphasize: "Our differences make us interesting!"
Closing (3 minutes)

Share one thing you learned about a classmate's preferences

Wednesday: Things I'm Good At

Opening Circle (5 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What are you really good at doing?"

  • Each child shares one thing they do well
  • Accept all answers: drawing, running, helping, reading
  • Celebrate each talent: "Wow! That's special!"
  • Make a class "Talents" chart
Abilities and Talents (12 minutes)

Talent Categories:

  • Physical: running, jumping, drawing, building
  • Mental: counting, reading, remembering, solving puzzles
  • Social: helping friends, sharing, making others laugh
  • Creative: singing, dancing, telling stories, making art

Individual Talent Books:

  • Children create "Things I'm Good At" books
  • Draw pictures of themselves doing their talents
  • Write simple sentences: "I am good at..."
  • Include at least 3 different abilities
Talent Show Planning (12 minutes)

Sharing Our Talents:

  • Each child chooses one talent to demonstrate
  • Plan mini-demonstrations for Friday
  • Practice: hopping, singing, showing artwork
  • Build excitement for sharing special abilities
Closing (3 minutes)

Affirmation: "Everyone has special talents that make them important"

Thursday: My Personal Information

Opening Circle (5 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What is your full name?"

  • Practice saying full names clearly
  • Discuss: first name, last name, family name
  • Share why their name is special
  • Count letters in different names
Important Information (12 minutes)

Personal Data Practice:

  • Full Name: Practice writing first and last name
  • Age: "I am ___ years old"
  • Birthday: Month and day (if known)
  • Address: House number and street (basic level)
  • Phone: Parents' phone number (practice saying digits)

Safety Connection:

  • Explain why we need to know this information
  • Practice for safety: if lost, can tell adults who they are
  • Role-play: "What would you tell a police officer?"
  • Emphasize: only share with trusted adults
Personal Information Cards (12 minutes)

Making ID Cards:

  • Create simple personal information cards
  • Include: name, age, drawing of self
  • Practice reading their information aloud
  • Decorate with favorite colors and designs
Closing (3 minutes)

Practice introducing self with full name and age

Friday: Celebrating Our Uniqueness

Opening Circle (5 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What makes you special and unique?"

  • Children share what they learned about themselves this week
  • Review: appearance, preferences, talents, personal information
  • Celebrate the diversity in the classroom
Mini Talent Show (12 minutes)

Sharing Our Special Abilities:

  • Each child demonstrates one talent (30 seconds each)
  • Possible demonstrations: jumping, drawing, singing, counting
  • Audience gives encouraging applause
  • Teacher highlights each child's unique contribution
  • Take photos for class memory book
All About Me Museum (12 minutes)

Displaying Our Work:

  • Set up displays of all week's activities
  • Self-portraits, favorite things charts, talent books
  • Personal information cards (keep safe)
  • Class charts showing similarities and differences
  • Gallery walk to appreciate everyone's uniqueness
Closing (3 minutes)

Group affirmation: "We are all special and unique individuals who make our class better!"

Preview next week: "Next week we'll learn about our families and how they help us grow!"

Assessment and Evaluation

What to Look For

  • Self-Awareness: Can the child describe their own characteristics accurately?
  • Personal Information: Does the child know their full name and age?
  • Preferences: Can the child express likes and dislikes with reasons?
  • Talents Recognition: Does the child identify personal strengths and abilities?
  • Respect for Differences: Does the child appreciate classmates' uniqueness?
  • Self-Confidence: Does the child show pride in their individual qualities?

Success Indicators

Meets Expectations:

  • Describes basic physical characteristics (eye color, hair color)
  • Knows and can say their full name and age
  • Identifies at least 2 things they like and 1 talent
  • Shows respect when learning about classmates
  • Participates willingly in sharing activities

Exceeds Expectations:

  • Describes detailed personal characteristics with confidence
  • Knows additional personal information (address, birthday)
  • Articulates clear reasons for preferences
  • Identifies multiple talents and abilities
  • Helps others feel good about their uniqueness

Extension Activities for Home

Family Identity Exploration

  • Family Interviews: Ask family members about their favorites and talents
  • Baby Pictures: Look at baby photos and discuss how you've grown
  • Name Stories: Learn about how you got your name and what it means
  • Talent Practice: Continue developing the talents discovered this week
  • Address Practice: Practice memorizing home address for safety
  • Special Collections: Start collecting things that represent your interests

Connecting to Objectivist Principles

Using Your Mind (Reason)

Children learn to observe and think about themselves objectively. They use evidence (what they see in mirrors, what they can do) to understand their own characteristics and abilities. This builds rational self-knowledge based on facts, not feelings.

Learning Real Things (Reality)

All self-discovery is based on observable reality - actual eye color, real talents they can demonstrate, preferences they truly feel. Children learn that their identity is based on real, objective characteristics, not on what others say about them.

Learning Helps Me (Self-Interest)

Understanding themselves helps children make better choices, communicate their needs, and develop their talents. They see how self-knowledge empowers them to pursue what they enjoy and excel at what they do well.

I Am Important (Individual Worth)

This entire week celebrates each child as a unique individual worthy of respect and appreciation. Every child's characteristics, preferences, and talents are valued equally. They learn that being different from others is not just okay - it's valuable and important.
