Week 1 Overview

This foundational week introduces students to the first five letters of the alphabet: A, B, C, D, and E. Children will learn to recognize these letters in both uppercase and lowercase forms, practice proper letter formation, and understand that letters have names and make sounds. This systematic approach builds the essential foundation for all future reading success.

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize letters A, B, C, D, E in uppercase and lowercase forms
  • Write letters A-E with proper formation and direction
  • Know letter names and say them clearly
  • Understand alphabetical order for the first five letters
  • Identify beginning sounds /a/, /b/, /c/, /d/, /e/ in spoken words
  • Connect letters to sounds they represent

Materials Needed This Week

  • Letter cards A-E (uppercase and lowercase)
  • Alphabet chart or poster
  • Letter formation worksheets
  • Sand/salt trays for letter tracing
  • Magnetic letters A-E
  • Picture cards for words beginning with A-E
  • Whiteboard and markers
  • Letter stamps or stickers
  • Reading journals or paper

Daily Lesson Plans

Monday: Introducing Letters A and B

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Question of the Day: "What letter does your name start with?"

  • Sing the alphabet song together
  • Point to letters A and B on alphabet chart
  • Show large letter cards: "This is A, this is B"
Letter Introduction (20 minutes)

Letter A:

  • Show uppercase A: "This is capital A"
  • Show lowercase a: "This is small a"
  • Trace A in the air with finger
  • Find things that start with A: apple, ant, alligator
  • Listen for /a/ sound at beginning of words

Letter B:

  • Show uppercase B: "This is capital B"
  • Show lowercase b: "This is small b"
  • Trace B in the air with finger
  • Find things that start with B: ball, book, bird
  • Listen for /b/ sound at beginning of words
Hands-On Practice (15 minutes)

Letter formation practice:

  • Trace A and B in sand/salt trays
  • Use finger to trace large letters on paper
  • Practice writing A and B on whiteboard
  • Say letter name while writing: "A... B..."
Closing (5 minutes)

Review: Hold up letter cards A and B, children name them

Tuesday: Introducing Letters C and D

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Review: "Who remembers letters A and B?"

  • Show A and B cards for quick recognition
  • Practice A and B sounds: /a/, /b/
  • Preview: "Today we learn C and D!"
Letter Introduction (20 minutes)

Letter C:

  • Show uppercase C: "This is capital C"
  • Show lowercase c: "This is small c"
  • Notice: "C looks like part of a circle"
  • Find things that start with C: cat, car, cookie
  • Listen for /c/ sound at beginning of words

Letter D:

  • Show uppercase D: "This is capital D"
  • Show lowercase d: "This is small d"
  • Notice: "D has a straight line and a curve"
  • Find things that start with D: dog, door, duck
  • Listen for /d/ sound at beginning of words
Letter Sorting Game (15 minutes)

Mix up letters A, B, C, D:

  • Put magnetic letters in a basket
  • Children take turns picking one letter
  • Name the letter and place it in correct spot
  • Line them up in order: A, B, C, D
Closing (5 minutes)

Practice alphabet order: A, B, C, D - what comes next?

Wednesday: Introducing Letter E and Review

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Quick Review: Flash cards for A, B, C, D

  • Children name each letter as shown
  • Practice sounds: /a/, /b/, /c/, /d/
  • Count: "We know 4 letters! Today we learn the 5th!"
Letter E Introduction (15 minutes)

Letter E:

  • Show uppercase E: "This is capital E"
  • Show lowercase e: "This is small e"
  • Notice: "E has three lines going across"
  • Find things that start with E: elephant, egg, elbow
  • Listen for /e/ sound at beginning of words
Letter Formation Practice (20 minutes)

Systematic writing practice for all letters A-E:

  • Demonstrate proper formation on board
  • Children practice in journals: A, B, C, D, E
  • Focus on starting at the top
  • Say letter name while writing
  • Circle their best letter in each row
Closing (5 minutes)

Celebrate: "We know 5 letters now! A, B, C, D, E!"

Thursday: Sound-Letter Connections

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Letter-Sound Review:

  • Show letter A, children say /a/
  • Show letter B, children say /b/
  • Continue with C, D, E
  • Practice: "A says /a/, B says /b/..."
Sound Identification Game (20 minutes)

Beginning Sound Sorting:

  • Use picture cards: apple, ball, cat, dog, egg
  • Say word slowly: "Apple... /a/... /a/... apple"
  • Children identify beginning sound
  • Match picture to correct letter card
  • Practice with multiple words for each letter

Word Examples:

  • A: apple, ant, airplane, alligator
  • B: ball, book, bird, banana
  • C: cat, car, cookie, cup
  • D: dog, door, duck, doll
  • E: egg, elephant, elbow, eleven
Letter Hunt Activity (15 minutes)

Find letters A-E in books and around the room:

  • Look through picture books for letters A-E
  • Point out letters in children's names
  • Find letters on classroom labels
  • Use letter stamps to make letter collections
Closing (5 minutes)

Name game: "Whose name has letter A? B? C? D? E?"

Friday: Assessment and Celebration

Opening Circle (10 minutes)

Letter of the Day Review:

  • Show all five letters learned this week
  • Children choose their favorite letter
  • Share why it's their favorite
  • Count how many children chose each letter
Individual Assessment (20 minutes)

Letter Recognition Check: (One-on-one with teacher)

  • Show uppercase letters A-E in random order
  • Child names each letter
  • Show lowercase letters a-e in random order
  • Child names each letter
  • Ask child to write letters A-E
  • Show pictures, child identifies beginning sounds

While Others: Independent letter activities

  • Letter coloring pages
  • Playdough letter making
  • Letter matching puzzles
Celebration Activity (15 minutes)

Letter Museum:

  • Display all letter work from the week
  • Children share their best letter writing
  • Sing alphabet song with special emphasis on A-E
  • Give out letter recognition certificates
Closing (5 minutes)

Preview next week: "Next week we'll learn letters F, G, H, I, J!"

Assessment and Evaluation

What to Look For

  • Letter Recognition: Can the child identify letters A-E in both cases?
  • Letter Formation: Does the child form letters correctly, starting at the top?
  • Letter Names: Can the child say the names of letters A-E clearly?
  • Sound Association: Can the child connect letters to their beginning sounds?
  • Alphabetical Order: Does the child understand A comes before B, etc.?

Success Indicators

Meets Expectations:

  • Recognizes 4 out of 5 uppercase letters A-E
  • Recognizes 3 out of 5 lowercase letters a-e
  • Writes letters with recognizable formation
  • Identifies 3 out of 5 beginning sounds

Exceeds Expectations:

  • Recognizes all letters A-E in both cases quickly
  • Writes letters with proper formation and size
  • Identifies all beginning sounds accurately
  • Notices letters A-E in environmental print

Extension Activities for Home

Family Letter Practice

  • Letter Detective: Find letters A-E on signs, books, and packages
  • Alphabet Books: Read ABC books together, focusing on A-E
  • Letter Writing: Practice writing letters in different materials (finger paint, sand)
  • Name Recognition: Help child find these letters in family names
  • Kitchen Letters: Make letters with cookie dough or find them on food packages
  • Letter Sounds: Play "I Spy" with things that start with A, B, C, D, or E

Connecting to Objectivist Principles

Using Your Mind (Reason)

Children learn that letters have consistent names and sounds. The letter "A" always looks the same and always makes the same sound. They discover logical patterns in how letters work and apply this reasoning to decode new words.

Learning Real Things (Reality)

Every letter is connected to real objects and real words. "A" for apple they can see and eat, "B" for ball they can hold and throw. Letters aren't arbitrary symbols but represent real sounds in real words.

Learning Helps Me (Self-Interest)

Children immediately see how knowing letters helps them: they can recognize their name, read signs, and understand books. Each letter learned is a tool that makes them more capable and independent.

I Am Important (Individual Worth)

Each child's progress with letters is valued individually. Some may master all five letters quickly, others need more time with three. Every child's learning journey is celebrated and supported at their own pace.
